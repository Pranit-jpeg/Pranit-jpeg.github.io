About

A short version and a long one.

Who I am, what I work on, and the small editorial obsessions I’d rather admit to up-front than have you discover in a meeting.

Short version: I’m a second-year MS Economics student at Northeastern University, graduating in May 2026. I came to Boston from India in 2024 for graduate school, and I’m looking for research, consulting, and policy-evaluation roles where the work starts before the regression does — in the data, the sample-selection notes, the weird footnote on page forty of the codebook.

Where I come from

I grew up in India and studied economics at NMIMS Mumbai before moving to Northeastern in 2024. My early training was heavier on theory than most American programs — lots of chalkboard optimization, less Stata — which turned out to be a strange gift. I learned to be suspicious of clean numbers before I learned to produce them.

Coming to the US shifted the question. Theory asks what should the parameter be? Applied work asks is this sample even the population you think it is? Most of my interesting mistakes since have lived at that seam.

What I’m working on

Most recently I was a Research & Evaluation Fellow at the City of Boston’s Department of Innovation & Technology, where I ran two difference-in-differences evaluations: the first on the MBTA fare-free bus pilot (a million-plus stop-level observations across 145 routes), the second on Boston Family Days, testing for chilling effects in non-English-speaking households after the program expanded from BPS to all K–12 schools. I also benchmarked the city’s labor-market performance against six peer metros for the economic outlook deck.

Alongside the fellowship, my coursework has leaned labor and applied micro — and my writing shows it. The two pieces I point people to first are a review and critique of Carvalho, Ferrero, Mazin & Nechio (2025) on demographics and real interest rates, and a referee report on Garrett, Ohrn & Suárez Serrato (2020, AER Insights) on bonus depreciation and local labor markets. The more technical work lives on GitHub: end-to-end replications of Card (1990), Dube et al., and Thistlethwaite–Campbell (1960); a full Berry–Levinsohn–Pakes random-coefficients logit with a Nevo-style cereal-market application; and an unsupervised-ML portfolio that includes a DBSCAN implementation I’m quietly proud of. I use R for actual analysis and Python for anything that touches a pipeline or a PDF. I know enough SQL to be dangerous and not enough to be paid for it — a gap I am fixing this summer.

On the side, I build small research infrastructure — the kind of unglamorous tooling that makes a study reproducible six months later. The most recent of these is JobPipeline, a local SQLite-backed queue that scrapes corporate career pages, scores each posting against four tailored résumés using the Claude API, and drops anything under a 75 % match. It is the single most-used program on my laptop.

How I work

A few operating commitments, for anyone hiring me or collaborating:

  • I don’t trust a model I can’t rebuild from scratch in forty minutes.
  • I redraw every chart at least once. Usually three times.
  • Stata for pedagogy, R for actual work, Python for everything else.
  • I read the codebook before I load the data. This is not normal; it should be.
  • I’d rather ship a small, clearly-labeled finding than a large one with a haunted standard error.
“The interesting stuff is almost never where the regression is — it’s two footnotes earlier, in the decision about which rows to drop.”

If your team does careful applied work and you’d rather have someone who writes clearly about a specification choice than someone who runs eight more, I’d love to hear from you.
